Programming Fonts

TL;DR
Computer programming requires a specialized form of reading. Programmers use a lot of different fonts for reading and writing code. In spite of this diversity, monospace kerning and lightly styled san-serif glyphs are almost universally prefered.

In many ways typography is the soul of graphic design. A good font must find a balance between style and clarity given the constraints of display/printing technology.

I recently had an opportunity to think about how type is used in computer programming environments. When confronted with an open space of possibility, I usually find basic research to be a helpful starting point. The results are not shocking but, I think these are worth sharing nonetheless.

The Data

The basic question of what fonts programmers use comes up every ~2 years on Hacker News [1], [2], [3], [4]. This offers a relatively rich dataset (n = 98), and it is relatively easy to collect and tabulate this data to get a sense of what sort of fonts people use when writing code. So what kind of typefaces do people use for reading and writing computer code?

As you can see there is a lot of variety when it comes to font preferences - allowing people to choose their own font is clearly a good idea for an IDE. There is probably some self-selection bias in this data. However people who care enough to change the default settings are also generally a good group to listen to.

While there is a lot of variety in the total number of fonts people use, a deeper look suggests that the diversity is largely superficial.

Kerning

Monospaced fonts are used by 96% of respondents. This preference can be explained since kerning would throw off the layout and visual formatting of code.

Serifs

Similarly san-serif fonts are strongly prefered by 90% of respondents. This may have to do with the need to read very small letters. While serifs may have a slight advantage in reading speed, for very small glyphs, serifs can become blurry.

While there is a strong preference for san-serif fonts, this does not mean helvetica clones are always appropriate. Certain glyphs (i, j, m, n) are often used idiomatically in programming. A good programming font needs to provide some additional ornamentation on these characters.

92% of the respondents used a font which had serifs on the lower case 'i'.

Conclusions

Computer programming requires a lot of reading (sometimes >8 hours a day). While traditional prose writing is concerned with large tracts of text, computer code focuses more on punctuation and formatting. A single character can change the meaning of a line of code, and indentation reveals logical structure. Looking at the preferences of computer programmers, a good font should:

  • Have monospaced letters
  • Have clean letters that are readable when small
  • Provide styling to help show critical glyphs
